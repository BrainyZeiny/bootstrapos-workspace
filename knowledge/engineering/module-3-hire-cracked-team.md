## Lesson 1: The Limits of Vibe Coding and When to Get a Developer

Let me start with something most vibe coding courses will never tell you.

Vibe coding has a ceiling. And you are going to hit it.

Every LLM has a context window. A limit to how much it can hold in its head at once. When your codebase gets complex enough — enough tables, enough components, enough features, enough edge cases — Claude starts losing the thread. It contradicts what it built three sessions ago. It breaks two things every time it fixes one. It generates code that looks right but does not work.

That ceiling exists today. It will rise as models get better. But it will never fully disappear.

BootstrapOS gets you incredibly far. Further than most founders think possible without a technical co-founder. But it is not infinite.

The second limit is time.

A good developer — a cracked remote developer who knows how to ship with AI — moves ten to fifteen times faster than you do with Claude Code. When you have paying customers your time has a real dollar value attached to it. At that point a developer does not cost money. It makes money.

The third limit is the one that kills the most founders.

If you are serious about building a SaaS business you need to be spending your time on marketing and sales. Not building. Not debugging.

This is the classic engineer conundrum. I was the developer at my own startup. I told myself I was being efficient. I told myself I was saving money. And I failed. Repeatedly. For years. Because I was building while the business starved.

Here is what makes this moment in history so dangerous. Everybody can code now. Claude Code exists. BootstrapOS exists. The barrier to building is gone. But it has created a brand new trap. Now instead of only technical founders getting sucked into the product, every founder can get sucked into the product.

You need to be one hundred percent heads up on marketing and sales. That is where revenue comes from.

So when do you actually hire.

You hire a developer when the codebase has outgrown what Claude can handle cleanly.

You hire a developer when you have revenue, time is the bottleneck, and the speed of building is costing you customers.

You hire a developer when you are serious about building a real SaaS business and you need to stop coding and start selling.

You do not hire a developer when you have no revenue. When you have not shipped yet. When Claude can still handle what you need.

Claude Code gets you to your first customer. A developer gets you to your first million. That is the division of labor.

## Lesson 2: The Mistakes That Cost Founders Thousands Before They Hire a Single Developer

I have had over 4,000 sales calls with founders over the past three years. And across 4,000 conversations I have seen the same mistakes over and over.

Mistake number one: hiring an agency.

Agencies are good at one thing: sales. They will get on a call with you and promise you the world. Here is what they do not tell you. An agency is a business. Their job is to acquire customers and maximize margin. So what you get is a product built by junior developers, managed by a project manager who has never shipped a startup, in a codebase you do not own and cannot maintain.

Never hire an agency to build your startup product. Never.

Mistake number two: letting someone else own your code.

Your codebase is your company. If someone else owns it — if the agency hosts it on their servers, if the freelancer keeps the repository private — you do not own your company. Always own your code. Always own your repository. Always own your deployment. No exceptions.

Mistake number three: thinking you do not need to be involved.

Building a differentiated product requires you to be deeply involved in what the product is. Not because you do not trust the developer. Because nobody understands your vision better than you do.

A developer builds what you describe. If you are not there to describe it clearly, in detail, every day, they will fill in the gaps with their best guess. And their best guess is never as good as your vision.

Mistake number four: hiring on Upwork without accountability systems.

Hourly billing on Upwork is essentially on the honor system. You are paying for time, not outcomes. Without a rigorous system for verifying what was actually built, you are flying blind.

What actually works.

You need a vetted developer. Not just someone who says they can build. Someone who has been tested, whose skills have been verified, whose track record is documented.

At RocketDevs we have spent eight hours vetting every developer on the platform. Technical assessments. Live coding exercises. Interviews. We publish the scores.

## Lesson 4: The Two Questions We Built Our Entire Vetting System On

After interviewing 300,000 developers we use two questions to vet every single one. Not ten questions. Not a five-round interview process. Two questions. And they tell us almost everything we need to know.

These two questions are designed so that a founder who has never written a single line of code can sit across from a developer — on a video call, in real time — and know within an hour whether this person can actually build.

Question one: Build me a Jira board.

You know what a Jira board looks like. Everybody does. Columns. Cards. Drag and drop.

You ask the developer to build one. Live. In front of you. Right now.

You are not evaluating the code. You are evaluating two things. First — do they actually build it? Second — how do they think out loud?

Everyone knows what a Jira board looks like. You can evaluate the output without understanding the code.

Question two: Leetcode. Two medium questions. Live.

Pick two medium difficulty problems. I would include LRU Cache as one of your two.

Leetcode tells you immediately whether the solution works. All test cases pass or they do not. You do not need to read a single line of code.

If a developer can solve two medium Leetcode problems live in front of you without help — they are a decent engineer.

Two questions. One hour. No bullshit.

## Lesson 5: How to Hire, Manage, and Keep a Developer Accountable From Day One

I was a principal engineer at TikTok. I managed teams that built products you have used a billion times. Before that I managed developers at American Express. I have been managing engineering teams since 2018.

Hire the developer who gives a shit.

You want the developer who emails you two or three times. Who asks questions before they start building. Who has opinions about how something should work and is not afraid to tell you. Who cares about your product almost as much as you do.

A brilliant developer who does not care about your business will do exactly what you tell them and nothing more. A developer who genuinely gives a shit will tell you when something does not make sense.

How to manage them once they are in.

Stand-ups are every single day. Ten minutes. What did you build yesterday. What are you building today. Is anything blocked.

Office hours are where the real work happens. The developer shows you what they built. You give product feedback. You go deep on the feature. You make decisions together.

Time accountability is non-negotiable.

Remote developers will be as lazy as you let them be. Not because they are bad people. Because that is human nature. You need a time tracking system. Screenshots. Clock in and clock out.

Transparency creates trust. When a developer knows their hours are tracked they never have to wonder if you think they are slacking. When you can see their hours you never have to wonder if you are getting what you are paying for.

Make it non-negotiable from day one. A developer who refuses time tracking is telling you something important about how they plan to work.
